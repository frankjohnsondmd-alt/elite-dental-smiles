Dark Tooth After Trauma: What It Means and When to Call

A tooth that turns gray, brown, yellow, or pink after an injury should be evaluated. Color change can be cosmetic, but it can also signal nerve damage.

Published July 1, 2026  |  Elite Dental Smiles

A dark tooth can be easy to ignore at first, especially if the injury happened months or years ago. Maybe you bumped a front tooth in a fall, took a hit during sports, or remember an accident as a child. The tooth may not hurt now, but the color has slowly changed. That change matters.

At Elite Dental Smiles, we see patients from Dandridge, Jefferson City, and across East Tennessee who are worried about one tooth looking darker than the rest. Sometimes the tooth is healthy enough and mainly needs cosmetic treatment. Other times the nerve inside the tooth has been damaged and the tooth needs root canal treatment, a crown, bonding, or another restorative option.

Why a Tooth Can Turn Dark After an Injury

Teeth are living structures. Inside the hard enamel and dentin is a soft pulp chamber that contains nerves and blood vessels. When a tooth is hit, the blood supply can be bruised or disrupted. Blood pigments can leak into the dentin and make the tooth look gray, brown, yellow, or even pink.

A darker tooth does not always mean the tooth is infected, but it does mean the tooth should be checked. Trauma can cause the nerve to heal, become irritated, or die over time. A tooth with a dead nerve may stay quiet for a while, then develop infection around the root later.

Signs You Should Schedule an Exam

Call a dentist if one tooth is clearly darker than neighboring teeth, especially after an injury. You should also schedule an exam if the tooth is sensitive to heat or cold, hurts when biting, feels loose, has swelling near the gum, or has a small bump on the gum above the root.

Pain is not required for there to be a problem. A traumatized tooth can lose vitality without causing major discomfort. That is why color change is worth taking seriously, even when the tooth feels normal.

Do Not Bleach One Dark Tooth Without a Diagnosis

Whitening strips and store-bought products often do not correct internal discoloration from trauma. More importantly, they can delay diagnosis if the tooth has nerve damage or infection.

How We Evaluate a Dark Tooth

The first step is a focused exam. We compare the color, check the gums, look for cracks or old restorations, test the bite, and evaluate how the tooth responds to temperature or other vitality testing. Dental X-rays help us look at the root and the surrounding bone.

We are looking for clues: Does the nerve still respond? Is there infection at the root tip? Is the tooth cracked? Has the root started to resorb? Is the discoloration mostly internal staining, external staining, or a combination? The answer guides treatment.

Treatment Options for a Dark Front Tooth

If the tooth is healthy but discolored, cosmetic bonding or a porcelain veneer may help blend it with the surrounding smile. If the tooth has had root canal treatment or needs one, internal bleaching may be considered in some cases. A crown may be recommended when the tooth is structurally weak, heavily restored, cracked, or needs more complete coverage.

If infection is present, the priority is controlling the infection and saving the tooth when possible. Root canal treatment removes infected or damaged tissue inside the tooth, seals the canal, and can relieve the source of the problem. After that, the final restoration depends on strength, appearance, and location.

Why Timing Matters

The earlier a traumatized tooth is evaluated, the more options you usually have. Waiting can allow infection, bone loss, root resorption, or fracture problems to progress quietly. Even if no treatment is needed right away, a baseline exam and X-ray give us something to compare against later. That record can be very helpful if symptoms or color changes appear months down the road.

For children and teens, trauma follow-up is especially important because developing teeth can change over time. For adults, the concern is often a front tooth that has slowly darkened and now stands out in photos or conversation. Either way, the safest move is to identify the cause before choosing a cosmetic fix.

When It Is Urgent

Call promptly if the dark tooth is painful, swollen, loose, draining, or associated with a bad taste. Also call if the injury is recent and the tooth moved, chipped, cracked, or was knocked out of position. Recent dental trauma deserves quick attention, even if you think the tooth might settle down on its own.

A single dark tooth is not something you have to live with or guess about. With the right diagnosis, many traumatized teeth can be treated comfortably and restored so they look natural again.
