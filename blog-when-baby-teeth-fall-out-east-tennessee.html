When Do Baby Teeth Fall Out?

A parent-friendly guide to loose baby teeth, permanent teeth, and when to call the dentist.

Published July 15, 2026  |  Elite Dental Smiles

Losing baby teeth is one of those childhood milestones that can be exciting, strange, and a little messy all at once. Most children start losing baby teeth around age 6, but the exact timing varies. Some kids lose their first tooth at 5. Others are closer to 7. A little variation is usually normal.

At Elite Dental Smiles, we see children from Dandridge, Jefferson City, White Pine, Morristown, and nearby East Tennessee communities for routine checkups and eruption concerns. The goal is simple: make sure baby teeth are falling out on schedule, permanent teeth have room to come in, and small concerns are caught before they turn into bigger bite or spacing problems.

Which Baby Teeth Usually Fall Out First?

The lower front teeth are usually first. These are followed by the upper front teeth. After that, the side front teeth, first baby molars, canines, and second baby molars follow over the next several years. Many children are still losing baby teeth at age 11 or 12.

A common pattern is front teeth around ages 6 to 8, first baby molars around ages 9 to 11, canines around ages 9 to 12, and second baby molars around ages 10 to 12. These are general ranges, not hard deadlines. What matters most is the pattern, symmetry, and whether the permanent teeth are coming in properly.

What If the New Tooth Comes In Behind the Baby Tooth?

Parents often call this "shark teeth." It happens when a permanent tooth erupts behind a baby tooth before the baby tooth has fallen out. This is especially common with lower front teeth. In many cases, the baby tooth becomes loose soon after and falls out on its own.

If the baby tooth is not loose, if the permanent tooth is coming in very far out of position, or if the situation does not improve over a few weeks, schedule a dental visit. Sometimes the baby tooth needs help getting out of the way so the permanent tooth can move forward with the tongue's natural pressure.

Do Not Force a Tooth That Is Not Ready

A very loose baby tooth can often be wiggled out gently. A tooth that hurts, bleeds heavily, or still feels firmly attached should not be pulled at home. Forcing it can tear gum tissue, leave root fragments, or make a child more anxious about dental care.

When Should Parents Be Concerned?

Call the dentist if one side is losing teeth much earlier than the other, if a baby tooth is still present long after the matching tooth on the other side fell out, or if a permanent tooth seems blocked. Also call if a loose tooth is painful, swollen, draining, or was loosened by a fall or sports injury.

Delayed tooth loss can happen for several reasons. Sometimes the permanent tooth is simply late. Sometimes there is crowding, an extra tooth, a missing permanent tooth, or a baby tooth root that is not dissolving normally. X-rays can help us see what is happening below the gums.

Why Baby Teeth Still Matter

Baby teeth are not throwaway teeth. They help children chew, speak clearly, smile confidently, and hold space for permanent teeth. A baby tooth with a cavity can become painful or infected. If it has to come out too early, nearby teeth may drift into the space and make it harder for the permanent tooth to erupt correctly.

That is why regular dental visits matter even during the years when teeth are falling out. We are watching more than cavities. We are checking growth, bite development, spacing, brushing habits, and whether the next teeth are on track.

How to Help at Home

Encourage your child to wiggle a loose tooth gently with a clean finger or tongue. Keep brushing around it, even if the area feels tender. Soft foods can help when chewing feels awkward. If a tooth comes out and there is a little bleeding, have your child bite gently on clean gauze or tissue for a few minutes.

Avoid tying floss to a tooth, using doorknob tricks, or turning tooth loss into a battle. The best approach is calm, simple, and child-led unless there is pain or a dental concern. Kids do better when they feel in control.

Common Questions About Baby Teeth Falling Out

What age do baby teeth start falling out?

Most children start around age 6, but age 5 to 7 can still be normal depending on growth and development.

Is it normal for permanent teeth to come in behind baby teeth?

It can be normal, especially with lower front teeth. If the baby tooth does not loosen or the permanent tooth looks very out of place, schedule a visit.

Should I pull my child's loose tooth?

If it is barely attached and your child is comfortable, gentle help may be fine. Do not force a tooth that hurts or feels firmly attached.

What if my child is late losing teeth?

A little delay is common. If no teeth have loosened by age 7 or the pattern seems uneven, a dental exam and X-rays can check development.
