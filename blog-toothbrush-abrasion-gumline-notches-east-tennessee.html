Toothbrush Abrasion: Why Teeth Get Notches Near the Gumline

Those small grooves near the gums are common, but they are worth checking before they become sensitive or deeper.

Published July 6, 2026  |  Elite Dental Smiles

If you run your tongue along the outside of your teeth and feel a little notch near the gumline, you are not imagining it. Many adults develop small grooves, scoops, or worn areas where the tooth meets the gum. Sometimes these areas are sensitive to cold air or brushing. Sometimes they are painless and only show up during a dental exam.

Patients often call this toothbrush abrasion, and brushing pressure can be part of the problem. But gumline notches are not always caused by brushing alone. They can come from a mix of brushing technique, gum recession, acidic drinks, tooth grinding, bite stress, and normal wear over time. The right treatment depends on what is actually driving the damage.

What Toothbrush Abrasion Looks Like

Toothbrush abrasion usually appears as a smooth worn area near the gumline, often on the cheek side of the teeth. The area may look yellowish because the enamel has thinned and the underlying dentin is showing through. Dentin is softer than enamel, so once it is exposed, it can wear faster and feel more sensitive.

These spots are commonly found on premolars and canines because they sit prominently in the arch and often take more brushing pressure. If gum recession is also present, the root surface may be exposed. Roots do not have the same hard enamel covering as the crown of the tooth, so they are easier to wear down.

Brushing Harder Is Not Better

A clean mouth does not require scrubbing. In fact, brushing harder can flatten toothbrush bristles, irritate the gums, and slowly wear tooth structure near the gumline. A soft brush, light pressure, and small controlled motions usually do a better job than force.

Electric toothbrushes can help, especially models with pressure sensors, but they can also cause problems if they are pressed hard against the teeth. Let the brush do the work. If your bristles look smashed after a few weeks, that is a clue that you may be using too much pressure.

The Simple Test

If brushing makes one gumline area zing, or if a notch seems to be getting deeper, schedule an exam. Sensitivity may be from exposed dentin, recession, a cavity, a crack, or bite stress.

Why Bite Stress Matters

Some gumline notches are related to the way teeth flex under pressure. Clenching, grinding, and uneven bite forces can place stress near the neck of the tooth. Over time, that stress may contribute to wedge-shaped defects, especially when combined with acidic drinks or aggressive brushing.

This is one reason a dentist may look beyond the toothbrush. If several teeth show similar wear, or if the notches line up with signs of grinding, a night guard or bite evaluation may be part of the conversation. Filling the notch without addressing the force may not hold up as well.

When Does a Gumline Notch Need Treatment?

Not every notch needs to be filled. Small, stable, painless areas may simply be monitored at regular visits. We may recommend a fluoride varnish, sensitivity toothpaste, better brushing technique, or changes to acidic drink habits.

Treatment becomes more important when the area is sensitive, trapping plaque, getting deeper, affecting appearance, or close to the nerve. In those cases, tooth-colored bonding can cover exposed dentin, reduce sensitivity, and make the area easier to keep clean. If gum recession is significant, gum therapy or a periodontal referral may be appropriate.

How to Protect These Areas at Home

Use a soft toothbrush and replace it when the bristles start to flare. Hold it with a relaxed grip instead of a fist. Angle the bristles gently toward the gumline and clean with short movements rather than long horizontal scrubbing. If you use whitening toothpaste, make sure it is not too abrasive for daily use.

Limit frequent sipping of acidic drinks such as soda, sports drinks, energy drinks, and lemon water. Acid softens tooth structure temporarily, and brushing right after acid exposure can increase wear. Rinse with water first and wait before brushing.

If you wake up with jaw soreness, chipped edges, headaches, or worn teeth, mention it at your visit. Grinding can be quiet and still cause real damage. Catching it early is much easier than rebuilding teeth after years of wear.
