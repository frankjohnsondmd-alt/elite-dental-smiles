Jaw Popping, Clicking, and TMJ Symptoms in East Tennessee

A clicking jaw is common, but it is not always something to ignore. Here is when jaw sounds, soreness, and bite changes should be checked.

Published July 10, 2026  |  Elite Dental Smiles

Jaw popping or clicking can be easy to dismiss, especially if it comes and goes. Many people in East Tennessee notice it when they yawn, chew a tough piece of food, sing, or open wide at the dentist. Sometimes the sound is harmless. Other times, it is a clue that the jaw joint, chewing muscles, bite, or teeth are under more stress than they should be.

The jaw joint is called the temporomandibular joint, often shortened to TMJ. You have one on each side, just in front of the ear. These joints work with muscles, ligaments, a small cartilage disc, and your bite. When that system is irritated or not moving smoothly, symptoms can show up as popping, clicking, locking, headaches, tooth wear, facial soreness, or pain near the ear.

Why Does a Jaw Pop or Click?

A jaw sound often happens when the small disc inside the joint moves out of its ideal position and then slips back as the mouth opens or closes. That movement can create a click, pop, or catching feeling. Clenching, grinding, trauma, arthritis, stress, missing teeth, uneven bite pressure, and frequent wide opening can all contribute.

Not every click means damage is happening. If your jaw clicks but does not hurt, does not lock, and your bite feels normal, your dentist may simply monitor it. The bigger concern is when the sound comes with pain, limited opening, headaches, muscle fatigue, or changes in how your teeth fit together.

Symptoms That Deserve a Dental Exam

Schedule an evaluation if your jaw gets stuck open or closed, if opening wide is difficult, or if chewing makes the joint sore. You should also call if you wake up with jaw tension, have frequent morning headaches, notice worn or chipped teeth, or feel pain around the ear when no ear infection is present.

Another warning sign is a bite that suddenly feels different. If one side touches first, if teeth no longer meet the way they used to, or if a crown or filling feels high, the jaw muscles may be working overtime to find a comfortable position. That extra strain can keep symptoms going.

Do Not Try to Force the Jaw

If your jaw is locked, catches hard, or will not open normally, do not force it wider. Avoid big bites, gum chewing, and tough foods until you are evaluated. Gentle rest is safer than trying to stretch through sharp pain.

How Teeth Grinding Fits In

Many TMJ related problems are tied to clenching or grinding. Patients are often surprised because they do not remember doing it. Grinding commonly happens during sleep or during stressful work, driving, workouts, or focused tasks. Over time it can flatten teeth, crack fillings, loosen crowns, inflame muscles, and overload the jaw joints.

A custom night guard may help protect the teeth and reduce muscle strain for some patients. The key is making sure the appliance fits correctly and matches the bite. A poorly fitting over the counter guard can sometimes make clenching patterns worse.

What Your Dentist Checks

At a TMJ focused dental visit, we look at more than the joint sound. We check how wide you can open, whether the jaw shifts to one side, which muscles are tender, how the teeth are wearing, and whether the bite is balanced. We also look for cracked teeth, failing dental work, gum inflammation, and signs of sleep related grinding.

Sometimes imaging is needed, especially if there has been trauma, major limitation, swelling, or concern about joint changes. In other cases, the first step may be conservative care, such as reducing strain, protecting the teeth, adjusting a high filling or crown, or making a custom appliance.

What You Can Do at Home

While you are waiting for an appointment, keep the jaw calm. Choose softer foods for a few days. Cut sandwiches and apples into smaller pieces instead of biting wide. Avoid chewing gum, ice, pens, fingernails, and tough meats. Use both sides to chew gently when comfortable, and try to keep your teeth slightly apart when you are not eating.

Warm compresses can relax sore muscles for some people. Short periods of cold may help if the joint feels inflamed. Over the counter anti inflammatory medicine may be appropriate for some patients, but only if you can take it safely with your health history and current medications.

When Treatment Is More Than a Night Guard

Some patients need more than a guard. If bite problems, missing teeth, worn teeth, or damaged dental work are driving the symptoms, the long term answer may involve restoring stability. That could mean repairing cracked teeth, replacing uneven restorations, treating gum or tooth infection, or planning a more complete bite solution.

The right plan depends on the cause. TMJ symptoms are frustrating because several problems can feel similar. A careful exam helps separate muscle tension from joint changes, tooth pain, bite trauma, and dental infection.

Do Not Ignore a Changing Jaw

A quiet click with no pain may not need urgent treatment, but worsening jaw symptoms should not be ignored. Pain, locking, limited opening, headaches, ear area soreness, or new bite changes are signs that your mouth is asking for attention.
