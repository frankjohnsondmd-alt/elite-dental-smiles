Why Does My Tooth Hurt When I Bite Down?

Biting pain is one of the clearest signs that a tooth, filling, gum, or bite needs attention.

Published July 19, 2026 | Elite Dental Smiles

A tooth that hurts only when you bite down can be confusing. It may feel fine while you are talking, drinking water, or brushing, then suddenly send a sharp signal when you chew on one spot. That pattern is common, and it usually means pressure is exposing a problem that is easy to miss when the tooth is at rest.

The good news is that biting pain gives your dentist useful information. The timing, location, and type of pain can point toward a cracked tooth, a high filling or crown, a cavity, gum inflammation, or an infection near the root. The sooner the cause is found, the better the chance of treating it before it turns into a bigger emergency.

Common Causes of Pain When Biting

A cracked tooth is one of the most common reasons a tooth hurts under chewing pressure. Cracks do not always show clearly in the mirror. Sometimes they are tiny, hidden under an old filling, or only painful when the tooth flexes during biting. Patients may describe a quick zing when they release the bite, pain on one corner of the tooth, or discomfort that comes and goes.

A high filling or crown can also make biting uncomfortable. If a restoration sits even slightly high, that tooth may hit before the others. Over time, the extra pressure can irritate the ligament around the root and make the tooth feel bruised. This is especially common after recent dental work, but it can also happen if teeth shift or if a crown wears differently than the teeth around it.

Decay can cause biting pain when a cavity weakens the tooth or reaches close to the nerve. In the early stages, a cavity may only react to sweets or cold. As it grows, chewing pressure can push on weakened enamel or inflamed dentin, creating a deeper ache.

Do Not Ignore Repeated Biting Pain

If the same tooth hurts every time you chew, schedule an exam. Waiting can turn a fixable crack, cavity, or bite issue into a root canal, extraction, or dental emergency.

Could Gum Problems Cause It?

Yes. Inflamed gums, a periodontal pocket, trapped food, or a localized gum infection can make a tooth sore when biting. The tooth may feel tender, slightly loose, or sensitive to pressure near the gumline. You might notice bleeding when brushing, swelling, a bad taste, or soreness that feels like it is between two teeth.

Food wedged between teeth can create surprising pain. Popcorn hulls, meat fibers, and small seeds can irritate the gum and make chewing feel sharp. Gentle flossing may remove the irritant, but pain that stays after the area is clean should be checked.

When Biting Pain May Be an Infection

An infected tooth often hurts when tapped or when you bite down. It may also throb, wake you at night, feel sensitive to heat, or cause swelling in the gum or face. A pimple-like bump on the gum, drainage, fever, or a bad taste can also point toward an abscess.

Dental infections do not reliably go away on their own. Antibiotics may calm swelling in certain cases, but they do not remove the source inside the tooth. If the nerve is infected, treatment may involve a root canal or extraction. If swelling affects breathing, swallowing, the eye area, or the floor of the mouth, seek urgent care immediately.

What to Do Until Your Appointment

Avoid chewing on the painful side. Stick with softer foods and skip hard items like nuts, ice, crusty bread, or chewy candy. Keep the area clean with gentle brushing and flossing. Warm salt water rinses may help irritated gums feel better. Over-the-counter pain relievers may help some patients, but follow the label and avoid anything your physician has told you not to take.

Do not test the tooth repeatedly to see if it still hurts. That can inflame the area more and make the pain harder to settle. Also avoid placing aspirin directly on the gum or tooth, because it can burn the tissue.
