Black Triangles Between Teeth in East Tennessee

Those small dark spaces near the gumline can be cosmetic, functional, or a sign that the gums need attention.

Published July 5, 2026 | Elite Dental Smiles

Black triangles are the small dark spaces that sometimes show between teeth near the gumline. Dentists call them open gingival embrasures, but most patients simply notice that their smile looks darker, older, or less even than it used to. They can also trap food and make flossing feel frustrating.

At Elite Dental Smiles, we see this concern in adults from Dandridge, Jefferson City, White Pine, Morristown, and surrounding East Tennessee communities. Some black triangles are mostly cosmetic. Others are connected to gum recession, bone loss, tooth movement, or older dental work that no longer fits the way it should. The right fix depends on the cause.

Why Black Triangles Form

Healthy gum tissue normally fills the small space between neighboring teeth. When that tissue shrinks, pulls back, or no longer has enough support from the bone underneath, a triangle-shaped opening can appear. Tooth shape matters too. Teeth that are more triangular naturally touch closer to the biting edge and leave more open space near the gumline.

Gum RecessionBrushing too hard, grinding, thin gum tissue, or gum disease can expose more root surface.
Bone LossPeriodontal disease can reduce the support that helps gum tissue stay full between teeth.
Tooth ShapeTriangular teeth are more likely to show dark spaces than square-shaped teeth.
Tooth MovementAfter orthodontics or natural shifting, contacts between teeth may change.

When It Is More Than a Cosmetic Issue

Black triangles are worth checking when they are new, getting larger, catching food, bleeding when you floss, or paired with bad breath or loose teeth. Those signs can point to inflammation or periodontal changes. Even if the tooth does not hurt, trapped food and plaque can irritate the gums and increase the risk of cavities along the root surface.

The exam is usually straightforward. We look at gum health, measure the spaces around the teeth, check for tartar below the gumline, evaluate tooth position, and inspect existing fillings, crowns, or veneers. Digital X-rays may be needed to see whether bone support has changed.

The Cause Matters More Than the Gap

Two patients can have black triangles that look similar but need completely different treatment. One may need gum therapy. Another may do well with cosmetic bonding. Another may benefit from orthodontic refinement or reshaping the tooth contacts. A good plan starts with diagnosis, not guesswork.

Treatment Options for Black Triangles

If gum disease or inflammation is part of the problem, we start there. A regular cleaning may be enough for mild inflammation, while deeper periodontal therapy may be needed when pockets and bone loss are present. Stabilizing the gums protects the teeth and helps prevent the spaces from getting worse.

For cosmetic correction, dental bonding can sometimes close small dark triangles by gently adding tooth-colored resin to the sides of the teeth. Veneers may be considered when the front teeth also have staining, wear, uneven edges, or shape concerns. In some cases, clear aligners can reposition teeth so the contacts are more favorable.

Not every black triangle should be closed completely. If a space is large, forcing material into it can create bulky teeth that are hard to clean. The goal is a natural-looking result that still lets you floss and keep the gums healthy.

How to Keep Black Triangles From Getting Worse

Use a soft toothbrush and gentle pressure at the gumline. Floss daily, but avoid snapping the floss hard into the gum tissue. If you grind your teeth, ask whether a night guard makes sense. Keep regular cleaning visits so plaque and tartar do not sit below the gumline and weaken the support around teeth.

If you already notice food packing into one area, do not ignore it. That repeat irritation is often what makes patients realize something has changed. Early evaluation usually gives you more treatment options and keeps the solution simpler.

Common Questions About Black Triangles

Are black triangles between teeth normal?

They are common, especially as gums change with age, but they can also point to gum recession, bone loss, tooth shape, or shifting teeth. An exam can tell whether treatment is needed.

Can black triangles be fixed?

Often, yes. Options may include bonding, veneers, orthodontic refinement, gum treatment, or reshaping contacts between teeth depending on the cause.

Do black triangles mean I have gum disease?

Not always. Gum disease is one possible cause, but tooth shape, previous orthodontics, recession, and natural anatomy can also create these spaces.

Why does food get caught in black triangles?

The open space near the gumline can trap seeds, meat fibers, and plaque. If it happens often, the area should be checked because food trapping can irritate gums and increase cavity risk.
