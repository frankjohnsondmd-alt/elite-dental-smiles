Plaque vs. Tartar: What Is the Difference?

Plaque and tartar are related, but they are not the same problem. Knowing the difference helps you protect your gums before small buildup becomes bigger dental work.

Published July 22, 2026  |  Elite Dental Smiles

Most people have heard a hygienist mention plaque or tartar during a cleaning. The words can sound interchangeable, but they describe two different stages of buildup on your teeth. One is soft and can usually be removed at home. The other is hardened and needs professional instruments.

That difference matters. Plaque and tartar are not only cosmetic concerns. They can irritate the gums, cause bleeding, contribute to bad breath, and increase the risk of cavities and gum disease. If you live in Dandridge, Jefferson City, White Pine, Morristown, or another East Tennessee community, regular cleanings help keep that buildup from quietly turning into a larger problem.

What Is Plaque?

Plaque is a soft, sticky film that forms on teeth every day. It contains bacteria, food particles, and saliva proteins. You may feel it as a fuzzy coating on your teeth, especially near the gumline or after a long day without brushing.

Because plaque is soft, brushing and flossing can remove much of it. The goal is not to make your mouth sterile. The goal is to keep bacterial buildup from sitting in the same places long enough to create acid, irritate the gums, and harden into tartar.

What Is Tartar?

Tartar, also called calculus, forms when plaque hardens on the teeth. Minerals in saliva help turn soft plaque into a rough deposit that sticks tightly to enamel, roots, and dental work. Once tartar forms, a toothbrush cannot remove it.

Tartar is often most noticeable behind the lower front teeth or along the gumline of the back teeth. It may look yellow, tan, or brown. It can also hide under the gums, where patients cannot see it but the tissue still reacts to it.

Do Not Scrape Tartar at Home

It may be tempting to use a sharp tool on visible buildup, but that can damage enamel, cut the gums, or leave rough areas that collect more plaque. Professional cleanings are the safer way to remove hardened deposits.

Why Buildup Causes Gum Problems

Gums are not supposed to live against bacterial buildup all day. When plaque and tartar sit along the gumline, the immune system responds with inflammation. Early inflammation is called gingivitis. Gums may bleed when brushing or flossing, look red, feel puffy, or become tender.

Gingivitis is often reversible with better home care and a professional cleaning. If buildup remains for too long, inflammation can move deeper. The gum attachment can weaken, pockets can form around teeth, and bone support can be lost. That is gum disease, and it requires more involved periodontal care.

How Often Should You Schedule a Cleaning?

Many patients do well with a cleaning every six months. Others build tartar quickly or have a history of gum disease, dry mouth, crowded teeth, smoking, diabetes, or dental work that traps plaque. Those patients may need cleanings more often to stay stable.

The right schedule is personal. If your hygienist keeps finding heavy buildup, bleeding gums, or deeper gum pockets, a shorter interval may save you from bigger treatment later. If your mouth stays healthy and your home care is strong, your dental team can help you maintain that progress.

What You Can Do at Home

Brush twice a day with fluoride toothpaste, aiming the bristles gently toward the gumline. Clean between your teeth every day with floss, picks, or small brushes that fit your spaces. If your gums bleed, do not quit. Bleeding often means the area needs more consistent cleaning, not less.

Electric toothbrushes can help many patients clean more effectively. So can a simple routine that you can actually repeat. The best technique is the one you will use every day without rushing.

Common Questions About Plaque and Tartar

Can I remove tartar at home?

No. Plaque can be brushed and flossed away, but tartar is hardened buildup that should be removed with professional dental instruments.

Is tartar the same as plaque?

No. Plaque is soft, sticky bacterial buildup. Tartar forms when plaque hardens on the teeth.

Why do my gums bleed when I brush?

Bleeding gums are commonly linked to plaque, tartar, and gum inflammation. A dental exam can determine whether gingivitis or gum disease is present.
