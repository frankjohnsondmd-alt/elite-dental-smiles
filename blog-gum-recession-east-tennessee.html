Gum Recession in East Tennessee: Why Teeth Look Longer and Feel Sensitive

Gum recession is common, but it should not be ignored. Early care can protect exposed roots and help prevent bigger dental problems.

Published May 21, 2026  |  Elite Dental Smiles

Gum recession happens when the gum tissue around a tooth pulls back or wears away, exposing more of the tooth or even part of the root. Many patients first notice that one tooth looks longer than the others. Others feel a quick zing from cold drinks, brushing, or sweet foods.

Recession can develop slowly, so it is easy to overlook until sensitivity or cosmetic changes become obvious. The problem is that exposed root surfaces are softer than enamel. They can wear down, stain, become sensitive, and develop cavities more easily.

Common Causes of Gum Recession

There is rarely just one cause. Gum recession often comes from a mix of habits, bite forces, gum inflammation, tooth position, and genetics. Understanding the reason matters because the right treatment depends on what is driving the tissue loss.

Aggressive BrushingHard brushing, stiff bristles, and abrasive toothpaste can wear gum tissue and tooth roots over time.
Gum DiseaseInflammation and bone loss can cause gums to pull away from teeth and create deeper pockets.
Clenching or GrindingHeavy bite forces can stress teeth near the gumline and contribute to notches, sensitivity, and recession.
Tooth PositionTeeth that sit outside the ideal arch may have thinner bone and gum support, making recession more likely.

Warning Signs to Watch For

Call a dentist if you notice teeth looking longer, roots becoming visible, new cold sensitivity, bleeding gums, bad breath, loose teeth, or small notches near the gumline. These signs do not always mean you need major treatment, but they do mean the area should be checked.

Recession is not just a cosmetic issue. When roots are exposed, plaque can collect more easily. Root cavities can form quickly, especially in patients with dry mouth, frequent snacking, or older dental work near the gumline.

It can also make routine dental work more complicated. A filling or crown placed near an unstable gumline may be harder to keep clean. If recession continues after treatment, sensitivity can return or the edge of the restoration can become more visible. That is why we want to know whether the tissue is stable before choosing the best repair.

Early Treatment Is Usually Simpler

The goal is to stop the cause before recession gets worse. A small sensitive area may only need home care changes, fluoride, bite protection, or monitoring. Advanced recession may need restorative treatment or referral for gum grafting.

What You Can Do at Home

Use a soft toothbrush or electric brush with pressure control. Brush gently at the gumline rather than scrubbing. Choose a non-abrasive fluoride toothpaste, and avoid whitening pastes if they make sensitivity worse. Clean between your teeth daily, but do not snap floss into the gums.

If you clench or grind, a custom night guard may protect teeth from heavy forces. If gum inflammation is present, professional cleanings and better plaque control are essential. If sensitivity is the main symptom, desensitizing toothpaste and fluoride can help, but the cause still needs to be addressed.

Patients sometimes wait because the area does not hurt all the time. That is understandable, but intermittent sensitivity can still be a warning sign. A quick exam can separate simple recession from a cavity, cracked tooth, failing filling, or gum infection.

Common Questions About Gum Recession

Can gum recession grow back?

Gum tissue usually does not grow back on its own. Treatment focuses on stopping the cause, protecting exposed roots, and discussing grafting or restorative options when needed.

Why are my teeth suddenly sensitive near the gums?

Sensitivity near the gumline can happen when recession exposes root surfaces. Cavities, cracked teeth, grinding, and worn enamel can also cause similar symptoms.

Does brushing too hard cause gum recession?

Yes. Aggressive brushing, hard bristles, and abrasive toothpaste can contribute to recession, especially when combined with gum inflammation or clenching.

When should I see a dentist for gum recession?

Schedule an exam if teeth look longer, roots are visible, sensitivity is increasing, gums bleed, or notches are forming near the gumline.
