Dental Bone Grafts: Why Your Dentist May Recommend One

A bone graft can help rebuild the foundation needed for a stable tooth replacement.

Published June 21, 2026  |  Elite Dental Smiles

When a tooth has been missing for a while, the jawbone in that area can begin to shrink. This is a normal response. Bone is maintained by use, and tooth roots give the jaw the daily stimulation it needs. Once a tooth is gone, the surrounding bone may slowly lose height, width, or density.

That bone loss matters most when a patient is thinking about a dental implant. An implant needs enough healthy bone around it to hold it securely. If the area is too thin or weak, your dentist may recommend a dental bone graft before placing the implant or sometimes at the same visit as an extraction.

What Is a Dental Bone Graft?

A dental bone graft is a procedure that adds bone material to an area where support has been lost. The goal is not simply to fill space. The goal is to create a scaffold that your body can heal into over time, improving the shape and strength of the ridge where a future implant may be placed.

Bone grafting materials can come from different sources depending on the situation. Your dentist will explain what type is appropriate for your case, why it is being used, and how long the area should heal before the next step.

Why Bone Loss Happens After Tooth Loss

Bone can change after an extraction, a long-term infection, gum disease, trauma, or years of wearing a removable denture. Some patients lose bone quickly after a tooth is removed. Others have a ridge that looks acceptable at first but is too narrow for ideal implant placement when measured carefully on imaging.

This is why implant planning should include more than a quick look in the mirror. X-rays and 3D imaging can help show the width, height, and position of the available bone. That information helps your dentist decide whether an implant can be placed predictably or whether grafting should be discussed first.

Think Foundation First

A dental implant is only as stable as the bone around it. Bone grafting can be the step that turns a questionable implant site into a more predictable one.

When Is a Bone Graft Recommended?

A graft may be recommended when a tooth is removed and the site needs to be preserved for a future implant. This is often called socket preservation. It can help limit collapse of the ridge and may make later implant treatment simpler.

Grafting may also be recommended months or years after a tooth has been missing. In those cases, the procedure may be used to rebuild a narrow ridge, improve support near the gumline, or create a better position for the final replacement tooth. Some cases need only a small graft. Others require more involved planning.

Does Every Implant Patient Need a Graft?

No. Many patients have enough bone for implant placement without grafting. Others need grafting because waiting too long after tooth loss changed the jaw shape. The only way to know is to evaluate the site, review your health history, look at the bite, and study the imaging.

If you are told you need a graft, it does not mean you are a poor candidate for implants. It usually means your dentist is trying to create the right foundation before moving forward.

What Should You Expect During Healing?

Bone graft healing takes time. The exact timeline depends on the size of the graft, the location, your health, and the treatment plan. Some patients move to implant placement after a few months. Others need longer before the area is ready.

During healing, it is important to follow instructions closely. Avoid smoking, keep the area clean, take medications as directed, and return for follow-up visits. Call the office if pain, swelling, bleeding, or a bad taste seems to worsen instead of improve.
