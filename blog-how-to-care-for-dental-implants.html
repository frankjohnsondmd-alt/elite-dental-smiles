How to Care for Dental Implants

Dental implants are designed to last, but they still need the right home care, professional maintenance, and bite protection.

Published May 8, 2026  |  Elite Dental Smiles

Dental implants are one of the most durable tooth replacement options available. They do not get cavities, they do not rely on neighboring teeth for support, and they can look and feel remarkably natural. But “permanent” does not mean maintenance-free. An implant can only stay healthy if the gum tissue and bone around it stay healthy too.

At Elite Dental Smiles, we want implant patients to think of implants less like hardware and more like high-quality dental work that deserves protection. With good daily habits and routine professional care, many implants can last for decades.

Clean Around the Implant Every Day

The implant itself cannot decay, but plaque and bacteria can still collect around the crown, abutment, and gumline. If that buildup is not removed, the tissue around the implant can become inflamed. Early inflammation is called peri-implant mucositis. If it progresses into the bone, it can become peri-implantitis, which is the implant version of gum disease and can threaten the stability of the implant.

Brush twice daily with a soft-bristled toothbrush or electric toothbrush. Angle the bristles gently toward the gumline and clean all sides of the implant crown. Floss daily as well. Depending on your implant shape and spacing, Dr. Johnson or your hygienist may recommend floss threaders, interdental brushes, a water flosser, or a specific implant-safe cleaning aid.

Protect the Implant From Heavy Bite Forces

Implants are strong, but they are not invincible. Natural teeth have a ligament around the root that gives them a tiny amount of movement and feedback. Implants are fused directly to bone, so they do not have that same shock absorber. If you grind or clench your teeth, the force can stress the crown, screw, implant components, and surrounding bone.

If you wake up with jaw soreness, headaches, worn teeth, or a history of broken dental work, ask about a night guard. A custom guard helps distribute force and protects both the implant and your natural teeth. This is especially important for patients with multiple implants, full-arch implant bridges, or a strong bite.

Do Not Skip Professional Maintenance

Implant maintenance visits are not just cleanings. We check the gum tissue, bite, crown, screw access, bone levels, and signs of inflammation. X-rays may be taken periodically to make sure the bone around the implant remains stable. If something feels loose, sore, swollen, or different, do not wait for your next scheduled visit. Small implant issues are usually easier to correct when they are caught early.

Your maintenance schedule depends on your risk level. Some patients do well with six-month cleanings. Others, especially patients with a history of gum disease, smoking, diabetes, or heavy tartar buildup, may need more frequent visits.

Call If Something Feels Loose

An implant crown, abutment screw, or bridge can sometimes loosen even when the implant itself is healthy. That is not something to ignore or chew on for weeks. If you notice movement, clicking, pressure, swelling, bleeding, or a bad taste around an implant, call us. Early treatment can often prevent damage to the implant parts or surrounding tissue.

Can Dental Implants Last a Lifetime?

They can, but longevity depends on biology, dentistry, and habits working together. A well-placed implant with healthy bone, a properly designed restoration, excellent home care, routine maintenance, and controlled bite forces has the best chance of lasting long term.

The best rule is simple: treat your implant like something valuable. Clean it every day, protect it from grinding, avoid tobacco, and let your dental team monitor it regularly. If you do that, your implant has a strong chance of serving you well for many years.

What to Avoid With Dental Implants

Avoid using an implant crown as a tool to tear packages, crack nuts, chew ice, or bite fingernails. Those habits can chip porcelain, loosen screws, and overload the restoration. Also be cautious with very hard or sticky foods if your implant crown is new or if you have a temporary restoration while the final crown is being made.

Good implant care is not complicated. It is consistency. The patients who do best are the ones who keep their cleanings, speak up early when something changes, and protect their bite before damage starts. That simple routine protects both comfort and confidence.
