What Counts as a Dental Emergency?

A dental emergency is any problem involving the teeth, gums, jaw, or mouth that needs prompt attention to relieve pain, stop infection, control bleeding, or save a tooth. Some issues can wait a few days. Others should be seen as soon as possible.

Severe ToothacheThrobbing pain, lingering sensitivity, pain that wakes you up, or pain with swelling can signal infection.
Broken or Cracked ToothA tooth that breaks, cracks, or loses a large filling should be evaluated before the damage spreads.
Dental Abscess or SwellingFacial swelling, gum swelling, fever, or a bad taste can mean infection and should not be ignored.
Knocked-Out ToothA permanent tooth that has been knocked out has the best chance of survival if treated within one hour.

What to Do Before Your Appointment

If you have tooth pain, rinse gently with warm water and floss around the area to remove trapped food. Avoid putting aspirin directly on the gum because it can burn the tissue. Over-the-counter pain relievers may help, but they do not treat the cause.

If a tooth breaks, save any pieces you can find and bring them with you. Avoid chewing on that side. If the edge is sharp, cover it with orthodontic wax or sugar-free gum until you can be seen.

If a permanent tooth is knocked out, hold it by the crown, not the root. If it is dirty, rinse it gently with milk or saline. Do not scrub it. Place it back in the socket if you can do so safely. If not, keep it in milk and call immediately.

When Swelling Becomes Urgent

Swelling is one of the signs we take most seriously. A small bump on the gum may still need prompt care, but facial swelling, fever, trouble swallowing, trouble breathing, or swelling near the eye can become dangerous. If breathing or swallowing is affected, go to the emergency room first.

Dental infections rarely go away on their own. Antibiotics may help control the spread temporarily, but the source of the infection usually needs dental treatment such as a root canal, extraction, or drainage. Waiting often makes treatment more complicated and more expensive.

Call Early, Even If You Are Unsure

The fastest way to handle a dental emergency is to call as soon as symptoms start. A small chip, early swelling, or toothache that comes and goes may seem manageable today, but it can become a much bigger problem overnight. We would rather help you make a clear plan early than see you suffer through a worsening infection or fracture.

How We Treat Dental Emergencies

The first goal is diagnosis. We listen to what happened, examine the area, take any needed X-rays, and identify whether the problem is decay, infection, trauma, a cracked tooth, gum disease, or bite-related damage.

From there, treatment depends on the cause. A broken tooth may need bonding, a filling, or a crown. A deeply infected tooth may need root canal therapy or extraction. A loose crown may be recemented or replaced. A painful wisdom tooth may need removal. The plan is built around stopping pain, protecting your health, and preserving the tooth whenever possible.

Common Questions About Emergency Dental Care

Should I go to the ER for a toothache?

For most toothaches, call a dentist first. The ER can help with serious swelling, fever, breathing problems, or uncontrolled bleeding, but it usually cannot fix the tooth. If you have trouble breathing or swallowing, go to the ER immediately.

Can a broken tooth wait?

Small chips may be able to wait a short time, but larger breaks, pain, sensitivity, or sharp edges should be evaluated quickly. Delaying care can allow cracks to spread or expose the nerve.

What if my crown comes off?

Save the crown and call us. Do not use super glue. Temporary dental cement from a pharmacy may help protect the tooth briefly, but the crown needs professional evaluation.

Do you see dental emergencies in Jefferson City?

How can I prevent future dental emergencies?

Regular checkups, treating small cavities early, wearing a night guard if you grind, and using a sports mouthguard can reduce your risk. Many emergencies start as small problems that become painful because they were not caught early.
