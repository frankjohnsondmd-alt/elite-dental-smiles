Temporary Crown Care in East Tennessee: What to Eat, Avoid, and Watch For

A temporary crown is meant to protect your tooth for a short time. The right habits can keep it comfortable until your final crown is ready.

Published July 20, 2026  |  Elite Dental Smiles

If you recently had a crown appointment at Elite Dental Smiles, you may leave with a temporary crown while your final crown is being made. That temporary is important. It protects the prepared tooth, keeps the space stable, and helps you chew and smile while the lab finishes the permanent restoration.

Temporary crowns are not built to last for years. They are made to do a short-term job, which means they need a little extra care. Most problems are avoidable if you know what to eat, how to clean around it, and when to call the office.

Why Temporary Crowns Need Special Care

A final crown is made from stronger material and is cemented for long-term use. A temporary crown is different. It is usually made from acrylic or composite material and attached with temporary cement so it can be removed when the permanent crown is ready.

That temporary cement is useful because it lets your dentist remove the crown without damaging the tooth. The tradeoff is that it is not as strong as permanent cement. Sticky foods, heavy biting, or flossing the wrong way can loosen the temporary before your next visit.

Chew CarefullyUse the opposite side when possible, especially for crunchy or chewy meals.
Avoid Sticky FoodsCaramel, taffy, gum, and sticky candy can pull a temporary crown off.
Brush GentlyKeep the gumline clean, but do not scrub aggressively around the temporary.
Floss the Right WaySlide floss out sideways instead of snapping it upward through the contact.

What You Can Eat With a Temporary Crown

Soft foods are safest for the first day or two. Eggs, pasta, soups, yogurt, tender chicken, fish, mashed potatoes, cooked vegetables, and smoothies are usually comfortable choices. Once the tooth settles down, most patients can eat a fairly normal diet as long as they avoid chewing hard foods directly on the temporary.

Foods to avoid include nuts, hard chips, ice, crusty bread, hard candy, chewy steak, sticky desserts, and anything that makes you feel like you have to pull or tear with the temporary crown. If the tooth is toward the back, it is especially easy to forget and bite down hard without thinking. Slowing down at meals helps.

Do Not Leave It Off

If a temporary crown comes loose or falls off, call us. The tooth can shift, become sensitive, or make the final crown harder to seat if it stays uncovered too long.

Is Sensitivity Normal?

Some sensitivity is normal with a temporary crown, especially to cold air, cold drinks, or pressure. The tooth has recently been shaped for a crown, and the temporary does not seal quite like the final crown will. Mild tenderness for a few days is common.

Call us if the bite feels high, if pain wakes you up, if swelling develops, or if sensitivity gets worse instead of better. A high temporary crown can make the tooth sore because it takes more pressure than the surrounding teeth. Adjusting the bite is usually quick, and it can prevent days of unnecessary discomfort.

How to Brush and Floss Around It

Brush the temporary crown like the rest of your teeth, but use gentle pressure around the gumline. Clean gums help the final crown appointment go smoothly. If plaque builds up around the temporary, the tissue can become inflamed and bleed when the permanent crown is seated.

Flossing matters too, but technique matters more. Slide the floss down carefully, clean the side of the tooth, then pull the floss out through the side instead of popping it back up. Snapping floss upward can catch the edge of the temporary and loosen it.

What If the Temporary Crown Comes Off?

First, do not panic. Save the crown if you can, and call the office. Avoid chewing on that side until we tell you what to do. Do not use household glue. It can irritate the tooth and gums, and it may make the final repair more difficult.

Depending on the situation, we may have you come in so we can clean the area and recement the temporary. That is usually the best option, especially if the tooth is sensitive or if your final crown appointment is still several days away.

Common Questions About Temporary Crowns

How long does a temporary crown need to last?

Usually only until your final crown is ready. The exact timing depends on the case, but the temporary should stay in place until your next scheduled crown visit.

Can I drink coffee with a temporary crown?

Yes, but avoid very hot drinks if the tooth is sensitive. Rinse with water afterward and keep the area clean.

Can a temporary crown feel rough?

It can feel different from your natural tooth. If it feels sharp, high, or uncomfortable when you bite, call us so we can check it.

Will the final crown feel better?

Most patients find the final crown feels smoother, stronger, and more natural than the temporary once the bite is adjusted and the tooth settles.

Temporary Crown Problem?

If your temporary crown feels loose, comes off, or causes sharp pain, call Elite Dental Smiles so we can help protect the tooth before your final crown visit.
