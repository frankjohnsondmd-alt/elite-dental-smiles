When Should Your Child First See the Dentist?

The American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry recommends a child's first dental visit by their first birthday, or within six months of the first tooth coming in. That is earlier than most parents expect, but the reasoning is straightforward: early visits are not really about cleaning a few baby teeth. They are about getting your child used to the environment, checking that development is on track, and giving you guidance on things like fluoride, diet, and pacifier habits that affect how teeth come in.

Waiting until there is a problem is the most common mistake we see. A child who first walks into a dental office in pain, for an urgent procedure, is going to have a very different experience than one who has been coming in for routine visits since age one. The first few visits are short, low-stakes, and designed to build trust. That investment pays off for years.

What Happens at a Routine Kids' Dental Visit

For young children, the first few visits focus on a gentle exam of the teeth, gums, bite, and jaw development. We check for early signs of cavities, note how the baby teeth are developing and spacing, and look ahead at what the permanent teeth are likely to do. We also review brushing technique with parents and answer questions about diet, fluoride use, and habits like thumb sucking or extended pacifier use.

As children get older and more cooperative, visits include professional cleaning, X-rays on the appropriate schedule, fluoride application, and assessment of orthodontic development. We do not push unnecessary procedures. When we see something we want to watch, we say so. When we see something that needs attention, we explain it plainly and give you options.

Dental Sealants: One of the Best Things You Can Do for Your Child's Teeth

Dental sealants are thin protective coatings applied to the chewing surfaces of back teeth, where most childhood cavities form. The grooves and pits on molars are deep enough that a toothbrush bristle simply cannot reach them. Bacteria get in, feed on food particles, and start the cavity process even in kids who brush regularly and eat reasonably well.

Sealing those surfaces when the permanent molars come in, typically around ages 6 and 12, closes off the problem areas before decay can start. The procedure takes a few minutes per tooth, requires no drilling, and is entirely painless. Studies consistently show sealants reduce the risk of cavities in treated teeth by nearly 80 percent over the first two years, with meaningful protection continuing for several years after.

We recommend sealants for most children on permanent molars as soon as they are fully erupted. If your child already has sealants from a previous dentist, we check their integrity at each visit and reseal as needed.

Handling Dental Anxiety in Kids

Dental anxiety in children is normal. Most of it comes from the unfamiliar, not from any real experience. The sounds, smells, and equipment are new, and a child's imagination fills in the rest. Our approach is to slow down, show before we do, and never force a child past the point where they are comfortable.

We introduce each instrument before using it. We explain what a sensation will feel like in honest, simple terms. We give children a sense of control by establishing a clear stop signal they can use at any time. Most anxious kids relax significantly within the first couple of visits once they realize nothing bad happens.

For children with more significant anxiety, or for procedures that are genuinely uncomfortable, we offer nitrous oxide sedation. Nitrous is safe, fast-acting, and the effects wear off within minutes after the mask is removed. It takes the edge off without putting a child fully to sleep. It is a practical option that makes many kids comfortable for procedures they would otherwise struggle through.

Cavities in Baby Teeth: Do They Really Need to Be Treated?

This question comes up often. The short answer is yes, in most cases. Baby teeth are not just placeholders. They hold space for permanent teeth, help children chew properly, and are important for speech development. An untreated cavity in a baby tooth can become painful, spread infection, and in some cases affect the developing permanent tooth underneath it.

That said, we look at the whole picture. A cavity in a tooth that is six months from falling out naturally gets managed differently than one in a molar that will not be replaced for several years. We do not treat unnecessarily, but we also do not defer things that will become bigger problems.

Common Questions About Kids' Dental Care

At what age should a child first see the dentist?

By their first birthday or within six months of the first tooth coming in. Starting early builds comfort, establishes a baseline, and gives parents guidance on habits and nutrition that affect how teeth develop.

Does Elite Dental Smiles accept dental insurance for children?

Yes. Our Jefferson City office accepts most major dental insurance plans, including many that cover children's preventive care at 100 percent. Our Dandridge location is fee for service. Call either office and we will verify your coverage before your child's appointment.

What is a dental sealant and does my child need one?

A dental sealant is a thin protective coating applied to the chewing surfaces of back teeth, where most childhood cavities form. It seals the deep grooves that are hard to clean with a toothbrush. Sealants are quick, painless, and can dramatically reduce cavity risk in school-age children. We typically recommend them for permanent molars as soon as they come in.

My child is anxious about the dentist. What can you do to help?

We see this often and take dental anxiety seriously. For most kids, a calm and patient approach with clear explanations makes the difference. For children with more significant anxiety, we offer nitrous oxide sedation, which is safe, fast-acting, and wears off within minutes after the visit. We never rush a child and always let them set the pace.
