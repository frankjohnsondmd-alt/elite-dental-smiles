When Should Your Child First See a Dentist?

The American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry recommends a child's first dental visit by age one or within six months of the first baby tooth appearing, whichever comes first. That recommendation surprises most parents. A one-year-old at the dentist feels early, but that is exactly the point.

The first visit is not primarily about cleaning. It is about establishing a baseline, identifying any early developmental concerns, and getting your child used to the environment before there is ever a problem to fix. A child who has been to the dentist four or five times before they need a filling is far easier to treat than a child who comes in for the first time because something hurts.

Early visits also give us a chance to talk with parents about diet, sippy cups, thumb sucking, fluoride, and the habits that have the biggest impact on a child's dental development. That conversation at age one or two can prevent significant problems at age six or seven.

Baby Teeth Matter More Than Most Parents Realize

One of the most common things we hear from parents is: "They are just baby teeth. They will fall out anyway." We understand the logic, but untreated cavities in baby teeth cause real problems.

Baby teeth hold space for the permanent teeth coming in behind them. When a baby tooth is lost early to decay or extraction, the neighboring teeth shift into that space and the permanent tooth underneath comes in crowded or angled. What could have been prevented with a small filling at age five becomes orthodontic treatment at age twelve.

Decay in baby teeth also spreads. An untreated cavity in a primary molar can reach the nerve, cause significant pain, and require more extensive treatment than a simple filling. And tooth pain in a child affects eating, sleeping, concentration, and behavior. It is not a minor inconvenience.

Keeping baby teeth healthy until they are naturally ready to come out is one of the best investments you can make in your child's long-term dental health.

Fluoride and Sealants: Two Tools That Work

Two of the most effective preventive treatments in pediatric dentistry are fluoride and sealants. Neither is complicated, and both have strong evidence behind them.

Fluoride. Fluoride strengthens tooth enamel and makes it more resistant to acid attacks from bacteria. We apply topical fluoride at each cleaning visit. For children at higher cavity risk, we may recommend a prescription-strength fluoride toothpaste for home use. Fluoride is safe, effective, and one of the most studied interventions in all of dentistry.

Dental sealants. The chewing surfaces of back molars are full of deep grooves that trap food and bacteria. Even a child who brushes well cannot fully clean those surfaces. Sealants are a thin protective coating applied to those grooves that physically blocks food and bacteria from settling in. The application takes just a few minutes per tooth and requires no drilling. Studies consistently show that sealed molars develop significantly fewer cavities than unsealed molars. We typically recommend sealants on permanent first molars (around age six to seven) and second molars (around age eleven to twelve).

Handling Dental Anxiety in Children

Some children walk into a dental office without hesitation. Others need more time, more reassurance, and more patience. Both are completely normal.

Our approach with anxious children is slow and predictable. We use a tell-show-do method: we explain what we are going to do, show them the instrument, and then proceed. Nothing happens without a heads up. We let kids hold the suction tool. We take breaks when needed. We do not rush.

For children with significant anxiety or for procedures that require more cooperation than a child can give, nitrous oxide sedation is available. Nitrous oxide, sometimes called laughing gas, is safe, fast-acting, and wears off within minutes. It takes the edge off without putting a child to sleep, and it is one of the most widely used tools in pediatric dentistry for a reason. It works.

Our Jefferson City office has treated children with severe dental anxiety successfully for years. If your child has had a difficult experience elsewhere, or if you know your child struggles in medical settings, please tell us when you call. We will plan accordingly.

When Do Kids Need Orthodontic Evaluation?

The American Association of Orthodontists recommends a first orthodontic evaluation at age seven. At that age, a mix of baby and permanent teeth allows an orthodontist or general dentist to identify crowding, bite problems, and spacing issues early. Many issues are better addressed before growth is complete, when treatment is faster and results are more stable.

We monitor your child's dental development at every visit. If we notice something that warrants an orthodontic consultation, we will tell you directly and explain what we are seeing. We do not refer unless there is a real reason to.

Common Questions About Pediatric Dentistry

When should my child first see a dentist?

The American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry recommends a child's first dental visit by age one or within six months of the first tooth appearing, whichever comes first. Starting early helps establish a baseline, catch any early issues, and gets your child comfortable with the dental environment before any problems arise.

What does a pediatric dental visit include?

A routine pediatric dental visit includes a gentle cleaning, a visual exam of all teeth and gums, X-rays when appropriate for age, a fluoride treatment, and a conversation with parents about diet, brushing habits, and any developmental questions. The goal is education as much as treatment.

Do children need dental X-rays?

X-rays are an important diagnostic tool for children because cavities between teeth are not visible during a visual exam alone. We use low-dose digital X-rays and only take them when clinically indicated based on your child's age and cavity risk. For most children, bitewing X-rays every 12 to 24 months is standard.

What if my child is nervous or afraid of the dentist?

Dental anxiety in children is very common and completely manageable. We use a slow, tell-show-do approach where we explain and demonstrate every step before we do it. For children with significant anxiety, nitrous oxide sedation is available to help them relax comfortably during treatment. Most children do much better after the first visit once they know what to expect.

Yes. Our Jefferson City office accepts most major dental insurance plans, including many plans that cover children. We recommend calling ahead to confirm your specific plan. Our team is happy to help you understand your coverage and what to expect at checkout.
