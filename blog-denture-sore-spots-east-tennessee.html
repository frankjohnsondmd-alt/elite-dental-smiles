Denture Sore Spots in East Tennessee: What Helps and When to Call

A denture should not keep you from eating, speaking, or smiling comfortably.

Published June 22, 2026  |  Elite Dental Smiles

Denture sore spots are common, especially after a new denture, a reline, or a recent extraction. But common does not mean you should just live with it. A tender spot can make meals frustrating, change the way you speak, and cause you to avoid wearing the denture long enough for the fit to become even harder to judge.

At Elite Dental Smiles, we see denture irritation in patients from Dandridge, Jefferson City, and surrounding East Tennessee communities. Most sore spots can be improved with a careful adjustment, but the right fix depends on why the denture is rubbing in the first place.

Why Dentures Cause Sore Spots

A denture rests on soft gum tissue and the bone underneath it. Those tissues change over time, especially after teeth are removed. Even a denture that fit well at first can start to rock, pinch, or rub as the gums and jawbone reshape.

Other causes include a bite that is hitting unevenly, food trapped under the denture, dry mouth, a crack or worn area in the appliance, or using too much adhesive to compensate for a loose fit. Sometimes the problem is not the denture itself, but an irritated area of tissue, a fungal infection, or a sore that needs diagnosis.

Do Not File or Bend the Denture at Home

It is tempting to sand down the spot that hurts, but home adjustments can create a bigger problem. Removing too much material can weaken the denture, change the bite, or shift pressure to a different area. Bending metal clasps on a partial denture can also damage the fit or break the clasp.

The better move is to mark when the soreness happens, where it feels tender, and whether it improves when the denture is out. If there is a visible ulcer, bleeding, swelling, or pain that keeps returning in the same place, schedule an appointment.

Quick Tip

If you are coming in for a denture sore spot, wear the denture for several hours before the visit if you can tolerate it. That helps the irritated area show clearly so the adjustment can be more precise.

What Helps Until Your Appointment

Rinse gently with warm salt water to soothe irritated tissue. Keep the denture clean, remove it at night unless your dentist has told you otherwise, and avoid hard or sharp foods that press into the sore area. If the denture is extremely painful, leave it out and call for guidance.

Denture adhesive may help a slightly loose denture feel more stable for a short time, but it should not be used to hide a poor fit. If you need more and more adhesive to function, the denture may need a reline, repair, or replacement evaluation.

When a Reline or New Denture May Be Needed

A small adjustment works when one area is rubbing. A reline may be needed when the inside of the denture no longer matches the shape of your gums. A new denture may be the better answer when the appliance is worn, cracked, unstable, or no longer supports your bite and smile properly.

For some patients, dental implants can add stability to a denture and reduce movement during chewing. Implant-supported dentures are not right for everyone, but they are worth discussing if a loose lower denture keeps causing sore spots or limits what you can eat.

Common Questions About Denture Sore Spots

Are denture sore spots normal?

Mild irritation can happen with new dentures, but persistent or worsening sore spots should be checked.

Should I adjust my denture at home?

No. Filing, bending, or trimming a denture at home can damage the fit and make the repair harder.

Why does my denture suddenly hurt?

The fit may have changed, the bite may be uneven, the denture may be worn, or the tissue may be irritated or infected.
