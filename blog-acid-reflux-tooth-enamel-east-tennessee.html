Acid Reflux and Tooth Enamel in East Tennessee

Heartburn is not just a stomach problem. Acid reflux can quietly damage teeth long before you feel a cavity.

By Dr. George F. Johnson III, DMD  |  Elite Dental Smiles  |  June 2026

Most people think of acid reflux as a burning feeling in the chest or throat. From a dental standpoint, reflux is also one of the more common hidden causes of enamel wear. Stomach acid is strong enough to soften and dissolve the outer surface of teeth. When that happens night after night, teeth can become sensitive, thin, yellow, chipped, or more prone to cavities.

At Elite Dental Smiles, we see reflux-related enamel damage in patients from Dandridge, Jefferson City, and surrounding East Tennessee communities. Many are surprised because they do not always have classic heartburn. Some have silent reflux, where acid reaches the mouth during sleep without obvious symptoms.

How Acid Reflux Damages Teeth

Tooth enamel is built to handle normal eating and drinking, but it does not tolerate repeated acid exposure well. When acid touches enamel, minerals begin to dissolve from the surface. Saliva can help neutralize acid and rebuild some mineral content, but it needs time. If reflux happens frequently, the mouth never fully recovers between episodes.

The pattern is often different from regular cavities. Reflux damage tends to show up on the inside surfaces of the upper front teeth, the biting edges, and the chewing surfaces of back teeth. Teeth may look smoother, glassy, or shorter. In more advanced cases, yellow dentin shows through because the white enamel layer has thinned.

Dental Signs

  • Sensitivity to cold, sweets, or brushing
  • Thin or translucent front teeth
  • Rounded or cupped chewing surfaces
  • Chipping along biting edges
  • Yellowing that does not whiten normally

Reflux Clues

  • Sour taste in the morning
  • Frequent throat clearing
  • Hoarseness or cough after lying down
  • Burning in the chest or throat
  • Waking with a dry or irritated mouth

Why Silent Reflux Gets Missed

Silent reflux is easy to overlook because the symptoms do not always feel digestive. A patient may mention morning hoarseness, bad breath, dry mouth, or tooth sensitivity, but not heartburn. During an exam, the enamel pattern can tell a story. If we see erosion in a way that does not match diet alone, reflux becomes part of the conversation.

This matters because placing fillings, crowns, or veneers without controlling the acid source can lead to repeat problems. Dentistry can repair damaged teeth, but the mouth needs a healthier environment for those repairs to last.

What You Can Do at Home

If you know or suspect you have reflux, start with simple steps that protect enamel. Do not brush immediately after an acid episode. The enamel surface is temporarily softened, and brushing too soon can scrub away more minerals. Rinse with water instead, wait about 30 minutes, then brush gently with a fluoride toothpaste.

Avoid sipping acidic drinks throughout the day. Coffee, soda, sports drinks, citrus drinks, and sparkling water can all add to the acid load. If you drink them, keep exposure brief and follow with water. Chewing sugar-free gum after meals may help stimulate saliva, which is one of the mouth's best natural defenses.

Medical Care Matters Too

Dental care does not replace medical treatment for reflux. If symptoms are frequent, severe, or affecting your sleep, talk with your physician. Reflux can be related to diet, weight, medications, hiatal hernia, sleep position, or other health factors. Getting the medical side under control is the best way to protect both your teeth and your esophagus.

How a Dentist Can Help

Our first goal is to identify the problem early. We check for enamel thinning, exposed dentin, cracked edges, bite changes, and areas where acid has left teeth more vulnerable. Fluoride treatments, prescription-strength toothpaste, and careful monitoring may be enough for mild cases. If teeth are already chipped or worn, bonding, crowns, or veneers may be needed to rebuild shape and protect the remaining tooth structure.

Some reflux patients also grind their teeth at night. Acid softens enamel, then grinding accelerates the wear. In those cases, a custom night guard can be part of the plan, but it needs to be used along with reflux control. A guard protects against tooth-to-tooth force, while medical and lifestyle steps reduce the acid exposure.

When to Schedule an Exam

Schedule a dental exam if your teeth have become sensitive, if the edges look thin or chipped, if whitening no longer works the way it used to, or if you wake with a sour taste or dry mouth. These are not problems to watch for years. Enamel does not grow back, so early intervention matters.
