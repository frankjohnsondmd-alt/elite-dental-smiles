Sinus Pressure and Tooth Pain in East Tennessee: How to Tell the Difference

Upper tooth pain can come from sinus pressure, allergies, or a true dental problem. The pattern of the pain gives important clues.

Published July 2, 2026  |  Elite Dental Smiles

East Tennessee allergy season can be rough. Pollen, weather changes, colds, and sinus congestion can create pressure across the cheeks and upper jaw. For some people, that pressure feels exactly like a toothache. It can be confusing because the roots of the upper back teeth sit close to the maxillary sinuses, so inflammation in that area can refer pain into the teeth.

Sinus-related tooth pain is real, but it is not the only explanation. A cavity, cracked tooth, infected nerve, failing filling, or gum problem can also cause upper tooth pain. The safest approach is to look at the pattern. Sinus pressure usually affects more than one upper tooth and often comes with congestion, facial pressure, postnasal drainage, or a recent cold. Dental pain is more likely to focus on one tooth, react to biting, linger after cold, or keep getting worse.

Why Sinuses Can Make Teeth Hurt

The maxillary sinuses are air spaces located above the upper molars and premolars. When the sinus lining becomes inflamed, pressure can build near the roots of those teeth. Your brain may interpret that pressure as tooth pain even when the teeth themselves are healthy.

Many patients describe sinus-related tooth pain as a dull ache across several upper back teeth. It may feel worse when bending forward, climbing stairs, or moving quickly. The discomfort may rise and fall with congestion. If both sides of the upper jaw ache at the same time, sinus pressure becomes more likely, especially during allergy season or after an upper respiratory infection.

Clues That It May Be a Dental Problem

Dental pain often behaves differently. If one specific tooth hurts when you bite down, release your bite, chew on something firm, or tap the tooth, it should be checked. Pain that lingers after cold or heat can point to nerve inflammation. Sensitivity to sweets may suggest decay. Swelling, a gum bump, bad taste, fever, or pain that wakes you up can point to infection and needs prompt attention.

A cracked tooth can be especially tricky. It may not hurt all the time. You might feel a sharp jolt only when chewing on one side or when the tooth flexes in a certain direction. Sinus medication will not fix that. Neither will waiting. Cracks and infections are easier to treat when they are caught early.

Quick Rule of Thumb

Pain across several upper teeth with congestion may be sinus-related. Pain in one tooth, pain when biting, swelling, or pain that lingers after cold is more likely dental and should be evaluated.

What You Can Try First

If your symptoms clearly match sinus pressure, consider supportive care for a day or two. Hydration, saline rinses, steam, allergy management, and over-the-counter medications may help some people. Follow directions carefully and check with your physician or pharmacist if you have medical conditions or take other medications.

While you are watching symptoms, avoid chewing hard foods on the painful side. Pay attention to whether the pain changes with head position or congestion. Also notice whether one tooth stands out. If the discomfort improves as your sinus symptoms improve, that is reassuring. If the tooth pain stays, becomes sharper, or localizes to one spot, schedule a dental exam.

When to Call Right Away

Call promptly if you have swelling in the face or gums, fever, drainage, a pimple-like bump on the gum, severe pain, pain that wakes you up, trouble opening your mouth, or trouble swallowing. These are not symptoms to ignore. Dental infections can spread, and early treatment is much better than waiting until the problem becomes an emergency.

You should also schedule an exam if tooth pain lasts longer than a couple of days, keeps returning, or changes how you chew. Even if the cause turns out to be sinus pressure, it is better to know than to guess while a dental problem quietly gets worse.

Tooth pain does not always mean a dental emergency, and sinus pressure does not always mean the teeth are fine. The pattern matters.
